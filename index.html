Our read

The platform has grown faster than the narrative. That's a good problem to have.

Stacklok today is a five-component enterprise platform with a new telemetry story, a Fortune 500 FSI proof point, and a CEO publicly repositioning the architecture. That's a lot of newness in a short window — and the external story hasn't had the chance to catch up yet.

The homepage does what most early-stage homepages do: it describes the product. Registry, Runtime, Gateway, Portal. Each component is accurate. What's still forming is the layer above the components — the named buyer, the problem in their language, the business outcome a Global 2000 CISO would recognize as theirs.

This isn't a critique. It's exactly where most technically-led companies sit right before they take the narrative leap. And it's the leap we help teams make.

What the work looks like

Three moves that would compound quickly.

We're not pitching a framework in the abstract. Here's specifically how we'd approach Stacklok's narrative in the first 60 days.

MOVE 01

Name the buyer. Name the problem. In their words.

We'd run customer and prospect interviews with CISOs, platform leaders, and AI governance owners at Global 2000 enterprises — the actual buyers for what Stacklok now sells. Not to validate what you already know, but to surface the exact language they use when they describe the "shadow MCP" problem to their boards.

That language becomes the top of your homepage, the first slide of your deck, and the opening line of every sales call. When a buyer reads their own words back, the sale gets shorter.

MOVE 02

Reframe the five components as one story.

Registry, Runtime, Gateway, Portal, MCP Scanner. Each is a real capability. Together, they're currently read as a product inventory. We'd reframe them into a single arc — the lifecycle a Global 2000 enterprise goes through when they bring AI agents inside the firewall.

The goal: a buyer who sees the five components and instantly understands which ones solve their current stage, and which ones they'll need next. That's how you turn a product page into a roadmap the buyer can sell internally.

MOVE 03

Extend the narrative into sales surfaces.

A repositioned homepage is the visible artifact. The leverage is in what happens behind it — the sales deck, the demo narrative, the competitive one-pager, the first-meeting opening. These are what Megan and the PMM team will need as they build out the function you've inherited.

We build the narrative system so it doesn't live in one place and die there. Your team picks it up and runs it across every surface the buyer touches.
